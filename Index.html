Chapter 01 · The Beginning

Born on the Streets of Bogotá

Before the startups. Before the millions raised. Before the football club in Portugal — there was a baby boy left at an orphanage on the streets of Bogotá, Colombia.

His mother, homeless and without means, made the hardest decision a parent can make: she gave him up, so he could have a chance at something better.

That baby was Brian York. Weeks old, he was adopted by an American family and brought to the south shore of Massachusetts. He grew up in Boston — the only "brown kid" in his neighborhood — always needing to work a little harder to belong.

Most people would have let that weigh them down. Brian let it fuel him.

Chapter 02 · The First Bet

14 Years Old. Already Building.

Brian didn't wait for opportunity. At just 14 years old, he launched his first business — a landscaping company with a close friend. By 16, they were operating heavy machinery. By college, they ran it remotely. Then they sold it.

That first exit planted something permanent in him: the knowledge that he could build something from nothing and turn it into something real.

He studied accounting, then pivoted sharply. Through timing and tenacity, he landed as CFO to Chris Sacca at Lowercase Ventures — one of the very first investors in Uber and Instagram. Brian was in the room where it happened.

Chapter 03 · Silicon Valley

Going All In. Every Time.

Silicon Valley sharpened Brian into an entrepreneur who was never afraid to lose. He raised a $3M seed round for Enthuse, a social network for sports fans. It failed. Two years of work, gone.

Most founders quit after their first failure. Brian discovered something about himself instead: he was exceptional at fundraising. He grew up playing poker — always going all in — and understood that in entrepreneurship, you either lose big or win big. There is no middle.

His next company, a mobile backend-as-a-service, he built and sold in just 24 months. His first real exit.

Chapter 04 · Back to the Roots

Returning to Bogotá. To Find Himself.

In 2017, something pulled Brian back. After years of Silicon Valley, he needed to return to where it all started. Colombia. The country that gave him life before he even had a name.

He landed in Bogotá knowing absolutely nobody. He had an idea — connecting truck drivers to shippers — and $100K of his own money to make it real. He cold-messaged strangers on LinkedIn. He met everyone he could. He followed up with every single person.

That startup became Liftit. In 2020, Brian led a $22.5M Series B, bringing total funding to $39 million. He had done it again — in a country he had no connections to.

In 2021, Forbes Colombia put him on the cover as one of the 30 Business Promises of the decade. The kid from the orphanage. On the cover of Forbes.

Chapter 05 · The Dream

The Man Who Wanted a Football Club

Somewhere between the startups and the pitch decks, Brian always carried one dream that had nothing to do with tech. He wanted to own a football club. Not as a vanity project — as a builder's project. The ultimate challenge.

He documented everything publicly. The business plan went online. The conversations with investors were filmed. He took his audience along for every step — the doubts, the due diligence, the deals falling through and coming together.

His content was pulling 300K+ views per video. Not because he was performing. But because people could feel this was a man who actually does what he says.

Chapter Six · The Arrival

Portugal. The Yellow & Black. AD Bobadelense.

In 2025, Brian York became a football club owner. Not in America, not in Colombia — but in Portugal, at a club founded in 1975 called Associação Desportiva Bobadelense.

From the streets of Bogotá to the football pitches of Portugal — Brian York has always been building toward something bigger than himself. The next chapter is just beginning.
